WEARDCYNN: Magical folk and eco-horror in the far future.

This document serves as the System Reference Document (SRD) for Weardcynn. It is licensed under CC-BY-SA 4.0

ᚠ. Foreword

NB: This is a work in progress.

Weardcynn (Weh-ARD-kin) is a tabletop role-playing game (RPG) made for one Game Master (GM) and at least one player. Players act as competent adventurers in a harsh and unforgiving world in search of lost treasures, forgotten knowledge, and any other tools that will help them and their kin survive.

The world of Weardcynn is what's left of a far-future earth that has risen up and overthrown its most persistent parasites, mankind, hereafter referred to as Manncynn. Manncynn still exist, but are much diminished, their primacy permanently hemmed in by the new champions of earth, the Weardcynn. These Weardcynn are themselves split between the hardliners who believe Manncynn cannot be trusted at all and those who have tentatively allied themselves with Manncynn on the assumption that their enemies can learn new ways. From these Weardcynn, the Manncynn have learned magic and other useful skills, which has helped them stave off extinction.

Manncynn settlements, ranging from highly portable hut villages to the musty remnants of old stone cities, are few and separated by vast stretches of hostile terrain: overgrown forests, salt flats and deserts, marshes and wetlands, and mountains whose passes are ever choked with snow. Exploration and discovery are still drivers of the human spirit, however, and while they negotiate their place among hostile equals, the Manncynn seek connections with one another and together unearth clues about who they once were from the long lost ruins of their dead civilizations.

ᚢ. Rules Overview

This is a brief overview of the major rules of Weardcynn.

Whenever a character's actions have an uncertain (i.e., interesting) result, you'll expend effort and roll dice. Combat, magic, leechcraft, and most kinds of actions performed in the wilderness or in dungeons require action rolls.

In Weardcynn, there are two concepts that will underpin all action rolls: Effort Pool and Ability Scores.

Action rolls are d20 dice pools. Select the ability score to use for the action roll, spend points from your Effort Pool to perform an action, then roll that many d20s. Success usually occurs when you roll under the ability score used on at least one of the d20s.

Your character's Effort Pool depletes as you perform actions. Resting replenishes the Effort Pool. Magic, leechcraft, and some other abilities can also help replenish Effort Pools.

When an action does damage, that damage is applied to an ability score. Armor and other means of defense subtract from the damage before it's applied.

When an ability score is reduced to 0 points, the character usually dies.

Only long-term rest can reliably restore damaged ability scores. During this long-term rest, the character must engage in specific activities outlined in their background. Less reliable methods of restoring damaged ability scores include magic and leechcraft.

Ability Scores

Player Characters have four ability scores whose values are randomly determined.

  • Body (ᛒ) governs all physical actions.
  • Mind (ᚦ) governs all mental actions.
  • Heart (ᚻ) governs all social/interpersonal actions.
  • Spirit (ᚷ) governs all spiritual actions.

When creating a PC, the player should roll 3d6 for each of their character’s ability scores, in order. They may then swap any two of the results.

Effort Pool

The Effort Pool represents a character's capacity to act in the world by expending effort. It is a resource that can be depleted and replenished.

Player Characters start the game with an Effort Pool of 11.

Possessions and Inventory

Characters have a total of 10 inventory slots: a backpack with six slots, one slot for each hand, and two slots for their upper body (such as the belt, chest, or head). The backpack can also double as an emergency sleeping bag but only if emptied of its contents.

Most items take up one slot, and small items can be bundled together. Slots are abstract and can be rearranged per the GM's discretion.

Bulky items take up two slots and are typically two-handed or awkward to carry. Anyone carrying a full inventory (i.e. filling all 10 slots) can no longer move or perform actions.

A PC cannot carry more items than their inventory allows. Carts (which must be pulled with both hands), horses, or mules can increase inventory. Hirelings can also be paid to carry equipment.

Actions

Actions are the core resolution mechanic of Weardcynn. Characters perform actions by spending points from their Effort Pool and making an Action Roll to see if they succeed.

Speed: Actions can be fast, middle, or slow in speed.

  • Fast actions occur in a single encounter round.
  • Middle actions take a dungeon turn.
  • Slow actions take a wilderness turn.

ᚪ. Magic and Leechcraft

Covers various kinds of magic, as well as leechcraft.

Runebinding

Largely the province of Runewitches, Runebinding is the act of affixing, or binding runes imbued with power to people, places, or things in ways that channel their power.

There are 24 runes. A rune's specific use depends on its orientation and the ability score used to bind it. Upright runes confer blessings while inverted runes confer curses.

Binding a rune requires a ritual that includes materials, words, and gestures. To bind a rune, perform the ritual and spend the required Effort Points to achieve the desired effect.

Runes can be bound as Fast, Middle, or Slow Actions.

When bound as a Fast Action, every failure on the Action roll deals a point of damage to the ability score used.

When bound as a Middle Action, each critical failure on the Action roll deals a point of damage to the ability score used.

When bound as a Slow Action, failure is harmless.

Once a rune is bound, it affects its until the effect resolves or it is unbound or dispelled.
